The AI Gold Rush: Are We Sacrificing Practicality for Innovation?
We’re in the midst of an AI gold rush. From toothbrushes to toasters, it seems every product is clamouring to be labelled "AI-powered." But is this shiny new object really what consumers crave? Let’s dive in. 
Remember when "IoT" was the next big thing? Every product, from your fridge to your lightbulb, was suddenly "smart." But let’s be honest, how many of those "smart" devices actually improved your life? I’m guessing not many. 
Now, it’s AI’s turn in the spotlight. We’re being bombarded with AI-infused everything. But as marketing guru, Jim Stengel, once said, "You can never know the customer too well." And it seems the message is clear: consumers aren’t buying the AI hype. 
A recent study found that people are less likely to purchase products labelled "AI." It’s like we’ve developed a collective scepticism. Maybe it’s because we’ve been burned by promises of technological utopia before. Or perhaps it’s because, let’s face it, most of the time, we don’t really need AI in our toothbrushes. 
Knowing Your Customer in the Age of AI:
Marketers have one golden rule: Know what your customers want. Sounds easy, right? Yet, it’s as elusive as finding a Wi-Fi signal at a tech conference. Procter & Gamble’s former CMO Jim Stengel once said, “You can never know the customer too well.” And he wasn’t kidding. He turned a simple dust rag into a billion-dollar brand with Swiffer, all because he knew what his customers needed. So, what do customers think about AI in their products? They’re not impressed. A recent study found that slapping an “AI” label on products often makes them less desirable. Emotional trust takes a nosedive when AI is mentioned, leading to lower purchase intentions. As Washington State University’s Mesut Cicek puts it, “Emotional trust plays a critical role in how consumers perceive AI-powered products.” 
The AI Effect: When “Smart” Becomes a Turnoff:
Let’s say you’re in the market for a smart TV. One group of people reads a description with the words “artificial intelligence,” and they’re like,“Eh, maybe not.” Another group reads the same description minus the AI mention, and suddenly, the TV is flying off the shelves. It’s like magic—except it’s not. It’s consumer psychology. This trend isn’t just limited to TVs. The effect is even stronger with high-stakes purchases like medical devices or pricey electronics. Consumers are wary, and who can blame them? No one wants to risk their hard-earned money on something that feels a bit too much like a sci-fi experiment. 
Yet, despite all this, some brands think the solution is to “demystify AI” and make it more accessible. But here’s the thing: Consumers aren’t confused. They’re just not interested. According to Forrester’s March 2024 Consumer Pulse Survey, only 31% of U.S. consumers have used generative AI, and 58% have heard of it but never tried it. And guess what? That’s the U.S., where tech adoption is usually faster than in other parts of the world. So, what’s the takeaway here? Brands should focus on making products people actually want. If AI can genuinely improve a product, great! But if it’s just a fancy add-on that no one asked for, maybe it’s time to rethink the strategy. After all, consumers care more about what a product does than how it does it. Remember, it’s simple—but not easy. 
When AI Brushes Up Against Reality:
Let’s talk about Oral-B’s latest innovation: an AI-powered toothbrush that analyses your brushing habits and points out the spots you missed. On paper, it sounds like a dream come true for anyone obsessed with oral hygiene. But here’s the catch—most people just want a toothbrush that does what it’s supposed to do: clean their teeth. 
Think about it. Brushing your teeth is one of those everyday tasks that doesn’t need to be overly complicated. You grab your toothbrush, scrub for a couple of minutes, and you’re done. The idea of an AI guiding your brushing routine might sound impressive, but in reality, it adds an extra layer of complexity that many consumers find unnecessary. 
The question isn’t whether the technology works—it likely does. The real issue is whether it’s something people actually want. For the average person, AI in a toothbrush feels more like a gimmick than a game-changer. It’s not that consumers are opposed to innovation; they just prefer practical, straightforward solutions to their everyday needs. In this case, a toothbrush that brushes well is often all they’re looking for. 
So, while Oral-B’s AI toothbrush might appeal to the tech-savvy crowd or those who love gadgets, for most people, it’s an example of technology being pushed where it’s not necessarily needed. Sometimes, simple is better, and that’s a lesson brands should keep in mind when designing the next big thing in consumer products. 
The Real Key to AI Adoption Lies in Understanding Consumer Needs:
The answer to the AI dilemma isn’t simply about simplifying the language we use to discuss artificial intelligence; it’s about genuinely understanding and addressing the needs and desires of consumers. The real challenge for brands is to move beyond the hype and buzzwords associated with AI and focus on developing products that deliver tangible value and enhance everyday experiences. 
Until companies recognise this fundamental truth—that the key to successful product adoption lies in genuinely resonating with their target audience—the much-touted AI revolution will remain a distant dream. As it stands, consumers are largely indifferent to AI features that don’t improve their lives or solve their problems. They want products that make sense in the context of their daily routines, not just products that showcase cutting-edge technology for technology’s sake. 
Brands that succeed in doing this will not only capture the hearts of consumers but will also pave the way for a more meaningful integration of AI into our lives—one that is welcomed rather than resisted. Until then, the conversation around AI will continue to be met with scepticism and disinterest, as consumers remain wary of products that seem to prioritise innovation over genuine utility.
